💰 Cost / getting the price down

  1. Is $3,300 your best price, or is there room? Any current promotions, seasonal, or sibling/referral discounts?

  4. Does paying in full upfront vs. installments change the price?
  5. Is there a lower-cost tier (group/cohort) that still produces a paper + mentor LOR? What exactly is lost vs. the $3,300 Individual?
  6. Any add-on fees beyond $3,300 (publication, extensions, materials, UCSD credit)? I want the true all-in number.
  7. Price-match: "We're also considering Polygence at a comparable price — is there anything you can do on cost or scope to make the decision easier?"

🎓 Mentor & academic fit

  1. How is the mentor matched? Can we see the mentor's profile / background / publications before committing?
  2. Can we request an astrophysics specialization (black holes, exoplanets, observational/data analysis)?
  3. If the mentor isn't a good fit, can we request a re-match — any cost or delay?
  4. What's the mentor's institution and degree level (PhD candidate vs. completed PhD — affects LOR weight)?

📅 Timeline & deliverables

  1. Exact start date and weekly cadence? Can the paper be finished by mid-August for fall apps?
  2. Concrete final deliverable — page length, format — and does the mentor help with revisions/editing?
  3. Will the mentor write a recommendation letter — included or extra? Any conditions?
  4. What publication or showcase options are in the base $3,300? Can he self-submit to JEI/Curieux without paying more?

📈 Outcomes & logistics

  1. What if he falls behind or needs to pause (school starts)? Extension policy?
  2. Refund / cancellation policy if it's not working early on?
  3. Roughly how many of your astrophysics students complete a paper, and any past project examples?
  4. Expected weekly time commitment (so we can plan around AST 111 + Python)?

🚫 NOT paying for: original research (a 17-year-old in 13 hrs isn't producing novel science, and that's fine) · a guaranteed publication (JEI accepts ~40%) · a measurable admissions edge (no public data shows Polygence kids get in at higher rates).

✅ ARE paying for: structure + accountability (deadline-driven framework that turns interest into a finished artifact by Aug 15) · a credible adult who can write a recommendation letter (genuinely valuable) · a Common App activity-list line ("Independent research on [topic] under Dr. X (Polygence)") · optionally a paper to link in Additional Info (Core Essential only).

❓ Six questions to ask before paying

  1. Will he actually do the work? If already self-directed → à la carte enough. If needs structure → Core Basic's PM earns its keep.
  2. Is the LOR the real deliverable? If yes → à la carte fine. Same mentor, same Zoom calls. Save ~$1,000.
  3. Does he already have 2 strong recommenders? If yes, Polygence LOR is nice-to-have, not essential — downgrades value significantly.
  4. 🎯 Has a UT cold-email reply landed? If a UT prof says yes (even tentatively) → skip Polygence entirely. A real UT astronomy lab credential beats any paid program. Single biggest variable. Polygence's own 2025 report shows just 1.1× odds at UT Austin (28.1% vs. 26.6% general) — their edge shows up at competitive privates, not your in-state flagship. Don't buy Polygence for UT.
  5. Could the same $4,200 buy something else? SAT/ACT 1:1 tutoring (~30 sessions, often higher direct impact) · 2× ASU credits + Harvard Ext ASTR E-1A + a paid lab subscription ≈ $2,500 · a used 8" Dobsonian + DSLR + tracking mount for real astrophotography · app fees + score reports + AP exams + college-visit travel ($2K–$5K in fall).
  6. Hidden cost: Polygence does NOT count as "demonstrated interest" at most colleges. Doesn't open doors at UT Austin. Signal value mainly at competitive privates where everyone has something.

🟢 vs 🔴: Wisely-spent vs Wasted

🟢 $4,200 wisely spent: uses every session · finishes the paper · gets a strong LOR usable for MIT/Caltech/Cornell · project becomes the spine of the "Why this major?" essay. If any of those three are doubtful, the money is better saved.

🔴 $4,200 wasted: attends 8/13 sessions · paper half-finished · mentor writes generic LOR · he barely mentions the project on the Common App because it didn't feel real to him.

Personal essay angle to develop

The strongest astrophysics-major essays are specific and intellectual, not "I've loved space since I was 5." Possible angles:

  • The moment a concept (e.g., the equivalence principle, dark matter rotation curves) clicked.
  • What he learned trying + failing to understand a real paper.
  • An unanswered question that motivates him (Why is the universe accelerating? Where are the missing baryons? What seeded SMBHs?)
  • The view of a specific object through a real telescope + what it made him want to understand.

📖 Why this plan — the philosophy behind every decision (tap to expand)

The 3 things that matter this summer

  1. A research or scholarly artifact you can name on the Common App ("I conducted X on Y under Dr. Z, producing a paper/poster/dataset/code")
  2. 1–2 strong recommenders who can write specifically about your astrophysics ability — usually a mentor, course instructor, or research advisor.
  3. Demonstrated college-level rigor — a transcripted college astronomy course or equivalent passes the "can he handle this major?" sniff test.

What does NOT matter much at this stage

  • Auditing free MOOCs (no proof of completion, no credential).
  • "Attended a 1-week summer camp" without an output.
  • Long lists of activities with no depth.

The realistic playbook

Pick ONE flagship summer commitment that produces an artifact + recommender (Flagship tab). Layer ONE academic course for transcripted rigor (Academic Core). Use local + free activities as supporting evidence of genuine interest. Then in August, turn everything into college app material (Fall Apps tab).
